The 5 Apps Every Solo Contractor Should Have on Their Phone (And Nothing Else)

June 18, 2026 6 min read

There are hundreds of apps marketed to contractors. CRM platforms, dispatching tools, lead management systems, GPS trackers, mileage loggers, inventory managers, payment processors, review generators, and marketing automation suites. For a solo contractor running 4 to 6 stops a day out of one truck, most of that software solves problems that do not exist yet and adds complexity to a workflow that needs to stay simple.

The solo operators who run the smoothest days are not the ones with the most tools. They are the ones who consolidated down to the fewest tools that cover the actual daily workflow, and stopped adding things. Here are the five that matter and why adding a sixth usually makes things worse.

1. A scheduling app that knows the addresses

This is the foundation. Everything else in the day flows from the schedule, so the schedule has to reflect reality. For a solo contractor, reality means driving between job sites, and the calendar needs to account for that driving, not ignore it.

Google Calendar handles the basics but treats addresses as notes rather than locations. It cannot calculate drive time, warn about geographic conflicts, or send departure notifications based on real traffic. It works for someone with one or two daily stops and predictable routes. It breaks down above three stops or whenever traffic is unpredictable.

Full field service platforms like Jobber ($39 to $199/month) and Housecall Pro ($49 to $189/month) include scheduling but bundle it with CRM, invoicing, dispatching, and team management features that a solo operator does not use. The scheduling component works, but paying $100+/month for it means subsidizing features designed for companies with employees.

CalenJob ($14.99/month) sits in the gap. Every appointment has a real address. Drive time between stops is calculated from Google Maps using live traffic at the actual hour of travel. That drive appears on the schedule as its own time-blocked event. When it is time to leave, a push notification fires based on real conditions, not a fixed reminder. Mileage is calculated automatically from the schedule. 2. A navigation app

Google Maps or Waze. Both are free, both provide turn-by-turn directions with real-time traffic, and both are essential. Pick one and use it consistently.

Google Maps has more accurate baseline data and handles multi-stop routing well. Waze is better at catching road closures and accidents in real time because its data comes from other drivers reporting as they go. For a contractor running 5 or more stops, Waze's community-sourced alerts can save 10 to 15 minutes per day by rerouting around problems that Google Maps has not detected yet.

The navigation app should be separate from the scheduling app. Scheduling tells you when to leave and how long the drive will take. Navigation tells you how to get there. Trying to combine both in one app means compromising one or the other. CalenJob handles this by letting you tap any job address to open it directly in your preferred maps app, keeping the two functions connected but separate.

3. An invoicing app

Getting paid should not require a laptop, an office, or 20 minutes of data entry after every job. The invoicing app needs to do three things: create a professional-looking invoice in under a minute, send it to the customer by email, and track whether it has been paid.

Wave is free and covers all three. The mobile app is clean enough to use between jobs, the invoices look professional, and the payment tracking is straightforward. For a solo operator in year one, Wave handles the invoicing need without a subscription fee.

QuickBooks Self-Employed ($15/month) adds tax categorization, receipt capture, and quarterly tax estimates on top of invoicing. Worth the upgrade once revenue is consistent enough that tax tracking becomes a real concern.

CalenJob's Business tier ($34.99/month) includes Stripe payment processing and invoice tracking directly inside the scheduling app, which eliminates the need for a separate invoicing tool entirely. But for a contractor on the Starter tier who is not ready for that, Wave or QuickBooks works alongside CalenJob without any conflict.

4. The phone camera

Not a separate photo documentation app. Not a job management platform with built-in photo capture. The camera that is already on the phone.

Before and after photos of every job are the most underrated business asset a solo contractor has. They resolve disputes about pre-existing damage. They provide documentation for warranty callbacks. They give the contractor a visual record of what was done, which is useful when a customer calls six months later and neither person remembers the specifics. They also make excellent content for Google Business Profile posts and Facebook group shares, which drives referrals without paid advertising.

The phone camera is free, already installed, and takes better photos than any built-in feature on any FSM platform. Take 2 to 3 photos per job (before, during, after), keep them in a folder organized by date, and move on. CalenJob allows attaching photos to individual jobs so they are linked to the customer record, but even without that, a simple photo folder on the phone covers the need.

5. The phone itself

The fifth app is not an app. It is the phone, used as a phone.

A solo contractor is the scheduler, the technician, and the customer service department. When a customer calls, the contractor either answers or loses the job to the competitor who picks up. When a customer texts to confirm the appointment, a quick reply builds trust. When a job runs long and the next customer needs to know, a 15-second phone call prevents a negative review.

The temptation is to add a CRM, an auto-responder, a chatbot, a virtual receptionist, or an AI answering service. Those tools exist for a reason, but the reason is usually a company with 10 or more inbound calls per day and an office coordinator to manage the flow. A solo contractor fielding 3 to 8 calls per day does not need a phone system. They need to answer the phone, respond to texts promptly, and call customers back within 30 minutes of a missed call.

Responsiveness is the cheapest and most effective marketing a solo contractor can do. It requires no subscription, no setup, and no learning curve. It just requires picking up.

Why adding a sixth app usually makes things worse

Every additional app introduces friction. A separate mileage tracker means remembering to classify trips at the end of the day. A separate CRM means entering customer data in two places. A separate review management tool means checking another dashboard. A separate lead management platform means another inbox to monitor.

For a company with office staff, that friction is absorbed by someone whose job is to manage tools. For a solo operator whose job is to fix things, every minute spent inside an app is a minute not spent on a job site or driving to the next one.

The contractors who run the cleanest operations are the ones who looked at their phone, deleted the apps they opened less than once a week, and consolidated everything that remained into the fewest tools possible. Five apps is not an arbitrary number. It is the practical limit for a one-person operation that needs to stay focused on the work.
