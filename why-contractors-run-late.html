The Real Reason Service Techs Run Late (It's Not Your Fault)

April 19, 2026 · 5 min read

Ask any customer why their plumber, electrician, or HVAC tech ran late, and you will get a character answer. Unreliable. Disorganized. Does not value anyone's time. Ask the tech, and you will get a structural one. The schedule was never going to work, the drive was longer than the calendar claimed, and the previous job took twice its estimate because that is what plumbing does. Both sides are frustrated. Only one of them is looking at the actual mechanics.

Late arrivals in the trades are almost never a time management problem. They are a scheduling infrastructure problem, and the infrastructure most service technicians are handed is built for a workday that looks nothing like theirs.

The calendar was built for office workers

Google Calendar, Outlook, Apple Calendar, and every tool built on top of them share an assumption: moving from one appointment to the next is free. A 10 AM meeting ends, you walk down the hall, and you are on time for the 10:30. The software does not need to know anything about distance, traffic, or geography, because for the person it was designed to serve, distance is a non-issue.

For a tradesperson driving between service calls, distance is the entire day. A 10 AM job in one neighborhood and an 11 AM job in another are not an hour apart. They are 47 minutes of work plus 18 minutes of driving plus a 15-minute gap that someone labeled "buffer," except the buffer is not actually a buffer. It is drive time wearing a disguise. The real workday has no slack in it at all, and nobody involved in scheduling it was aware of that.

This is why so many service techs end up apologizing at 10:47 for a 10:30 appointment. The calendar they were using does not know where anything is.

Drive time estimates come from feel, not from the road

The second structural issue is that nearly all drive time estimates in a typical service schedule come from memory. The tech has done the route a hundred times. The office staff booking the next job knows it takes "about half an hour." Those estimates are accurate on a good day, at a quiet hour, with no construction. They are wildly wrong during afternoon rush, during school pickup, on the Friday before a long weekend, and during the six weeks every summer when the city repaves something.

Traffic variance in most metropolitan areas is between 40 and 80 percent on the same route depending on the hour. A drive that takes 20 minutes at 10 AM can take 35 at 4:30 PM. Nobody in the schedule's creation factored that in, because the tools used to build the schedule have no awareness of the road. The 20-minute estimate gets locked in, the real drive takes 35, and the tech is now 15 minutes behind on the next job through no action of their own.

Job duration is a guess that pretends to be a plan

Compounding the drive-time issue is the reality of how service work actually unfolds on site. A water heater replacement booked for 90 minutes runs to two and a half hours because the shutoff valve is seized, the previous installer used non-standard fittings, or the customer asks about a leaking faucet upstairs and it turns into a second job. An electrical service call scheduled as a 30-minute diagnostic becomes a panel replacement once the tech sees what is actually behind the cover.

Every experienced tradesperson knows job duration is a best-case estimate, not a prediction. But the calendar treats it as a hard commitment, and the next appointment is booked against that commitment. When the job runs long, which is what service work does, the schedule has no elasticity to absorb it. The delay compounds at every stop. By early afternoon, a 12-minute overrun at the first job has become a 45-minute delay at the third.

The schedule assumes a perfect day. A perfect day has never happened.

What the industry's response has been, and why it has not helped

The field service industry's answer to this problem over the past decade has been to build larger and more complex scheduling software. Route optimization platforms, territory management tools, and full-service dispatch systems promise to solve scheduling chaos by replacing the calendar entirely with something more sophisticated.

For a 50-truck HVAC operation, those tools make sense. For the solo plumber, the independent electrician, the one-person pest control route, the handyman running a two-van shop, they do not. They are priced for enterprise, they require administrative labor to maintain, and they assume a scheduling department exists somewhere to operate them. The tradesperson who needs help the most, the owner-operator driving the truck and answering the phone, has been routed around entirely.

What most of these techs have, in practice, is Google Calendar. Or a paper planner. Or a shared family calendar app that a spouse uses to keep the kids' schedules in order. None of those know what a truck is, where the next job is, or how long it takes to get there at 4:15 PM on a Friday.

What a calendar for the trades would actually look like

The mechanical fix is not complicated. A scheduling tool built for service technicians would need to do four things that no mainstream calendar currently does.

It would treat every appointment as a location, not just a time. When a job is entered, its address becomes a first-class fact about the event, and the calendar knows where every appointment is relative to every other one.

It would pull live drive time from a mapping service at the hour of travel, not an average. If the drive from the 2 PM job to the 3:30 PM job takes 50 minutes during afternoon rush, the calendar reflects 50 minutes, not a memory-based 30.

It would render that drive as its own event on the schedule, visible and time-blocked, so that nobody (including the tech themselves) can accidentally book a third job on top of the drive that was supposed to connect the first two.

It would flag impossibilities before they become problems. If a 2 PM on one side of the metro and a 3:30 PM on the other are physically unreachable in that window, the calendar should say so at the moment the second job is being booked, not after the day has already fallen apart.

None of that is ambitious technology. Google Maps has offered traffic-aware drive time via API since 2013. The reason no mainstream calendar has integrated it is that the people building calendars do not drive between appointments, and the people who do drive between appointments have been treated as too niche to build for.

Why this matters for independent trades

There are roughly half a million plumbers and 400,000 HVAC technicians working in the United States, plus electricians, handymen, pest control techs, appliance repair pros, locksmiths, cleaning services, mobile notaries, and everyone else whose workday revolves around driving between appointments. The calendar infrastructure those workers have been using was not designed for them. Late arrivals, customer frustration, and lost referrals are the compounding cost of that gap. The fix is not better time management. The fix is a calendar that understands the road.
